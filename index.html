The beginning of AI

The birth of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in the mid-20th century, spearheaded by figures like Alan Turing and marked by the Dartmouth Conference in 1956, led to the development of early AI programs for reasoning and problem-solving. Despite setbacks, including "AI winters," the field surged forward in the 21st century with advancements in machine learning and deep learning, resulting in a diverse array of AI applications we see today

How does AI work

AI functions by processing data through algorithms to mimic human-like intelligence. It involves three main steps: data input, training, and inference. Initially, large datasets are fed into the system, which learns patterns and relationships during a training phase, adjusting parameters for accuracy. Once trained, the AI model can make predictions or decisions based on new data, without requiring explicit human programming for each task

AI helps with disablities

Artificial Intelligence (AI) empowers individuals with disabilities through assistive technologies like screen readers and speech recognition, facilitating access to digital content and communication. AI also supports personalized learning, autonomous transportation, healthcare monitoring, and mental health assistance, promoting independence and well-being. Augmented and virtual reality technologies enhance rehabilitation and skill-building exercises, demonstrating AI's broad impact on improving the lives of people with disabilities

AI - what's next?

The future of AI encompasses advancements in healthcare, autonomous vehicles, NLP, ethics, education, climate change, creativity, automation, governance, and AR/VR. These developments will revolutionize disease diagnosis, self-driving technology, language understanding, educational tutoring, climate modeling, creativity, industrial automation, ethical regulation, and immersive experiences, necessitating discussions around responsible integration and international collaboration
